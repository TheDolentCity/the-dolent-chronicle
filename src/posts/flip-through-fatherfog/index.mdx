*A _Flip Through_ is when I set aside some time to read through a new RPG. This is not a deep dive or formal review. I like to point out what pushes me to read further, what elements I think put me off on playing the game, and overall if it's a good idea for you to read it yourself. I don't edit them, so if there are mistakes I'm not liable.*

---

[_Fatherfog_](https://www.tuesdayknightgames.com/products/fatherfog0e) is a new fairytale horror RPG from Tuesday Knight Games, the designers behind Mothership RPG. This "Zero Edition" dropped a few weeks ago out of the blue. I'm a little surprised by the somewhat stealthy release. You can't even find it in the "New Releases" or "Tabletop Games" sections on the website. 

Whatever the case, Tuesday Knight Games appears to have continued the same strategy they used for _Mothership_. _Fatherfog_ is a "0E" that will likely get some small zines, supplements, and indie interest before crowdfunding a deluxe "1E" of some sort. But should you pick up a copy? Yes, you should, the [PDF is literally free](https://www.tuesdayknightgames.com/products/fatherfog0e?variant=43186386993241). I'll flip through the game for you though.

## Page 1 — The Cover

<Figure src={cover}
        alt="The Fatherfog RPG cover with several cloaked characters leaving a fog and approaching a tree with one hanging apple, drawn in a gritty, pencil style with only black, white, and moss green for color." />

The art style is striking. Its distinct from the messy, oily, Mothership style, instead evoking black-and-white pencil work you would find in a children's story. There is also this pale, moss color that makes up most of the background. It sets a clear mood to me. This is not a game of flashing red lights, yellow infectious goo, or bright pink murder androids: this is a game about the dark things in the woods outside your home. 

## Page 3 — Table of Contents

Looks like ~15 pages to create a character, ~15 pages to cover the rules, and ~15 to cover advice for the Guide (the unique GM term for this game I guess). The last entry in the list is "Despair". Always love me a good Despair.

An interesting note: neither Sean McCoy nor Luke Gearing are credited for any development at this stage. The game appears to be an Alan Gerding original.

## Page 5 — Introduction

An evil fog full of horrific things spread across the land. And even stranger, safe havens called foghouses appeared with enigmatic caretakers who blow a great horn to attract wanderers in the fog.

Player characters are "the few brave enough to venture into the Fog" with the hope of finding a dying resource: "Hope." Interesting premise. I'm not sure if Hope is a literal resource to be collected, or more of a metaphor.

## Pages 10 through 13 — The Four Classes

These are the same mechanical archetypes as Mothership, but the flavor and equipment is unique to the fantasy setting. You can play as:

- the **Worker**, someone with a skilled trade, boosted strength, and useful equipment.
- the **Philosopher**, an educated individual who specializes in some trade.
- the **Stranger**, an odd character who appears to be born of the Fog itself.
- the **Hunter**, a brave person who resorts to violence to feed their kin.

These archetypes are fine, but I think they could be stronger. Stranger is unique, Hunter is *necessary* for the genre, but Worker and Philosopher feel very mechanically driven.

I also don't like how a lot of the gear packs are handled. Do you want cool stuff unique to your class? Or do you want rations? If you pick cool stuff and your friend picks rations, do you split them?

Just standardize shared resources like food since gathering food will be a group activity anyway.

## Pages 14 through 16 — Cloaks, Brooches, and Trinkets

An awesome addition specific to Fatherfog. The trinkets and patch system from Mothership is now the cloaks, brooches, and trinkets system. Cloaks have a practical purpose (keep you warm, let others spot you), but also each cloak color has a significant social meaning.

        alt="A list of ten cloak colors and their descriptions."

They even have a detailed section for *how* a cloak is worn. Over a pack, under a pack, and rolled up (and why that's a bad idea).

I have nothing to say about brooches and trinkets besides they're neat. If you like the Mothership stuff, you'll probably like these tables too.

## Pages 17 through 19 — Coin

Hm. I know we live in a Capitalist, market-driven society, but I don't think isolated, "medievalish" communities in an apocalyptic magical fog would utilize coin. Where are these coins made? Who regulates them? Why does a coin keep value from one community to the next? I know it's probably not a big deal to basically to anyone else, but it would be *so easy* to have this world function on basic credit systems and then maybe utilize barter for the odd times that large communities find each other within the Fog. A d100 table of credit (who owes you and/or who do you owe) would be more unique and possibly even function as a campaign starter.

##  Pages 22 through 23 — Melee and Ranged Weapons

So I haven't gotten to the combat rules yet, but this appears to already have a departure from Mothership. Damage is based on, and usually equal to, your **WIL** stat. I would expect this to be a high number, so I'm a bit interested how that works in action. We'll get to that later I guess.

I know a lot of OSR texts have a lot of weapons listed with the same damage, but there is something funny to me about giving some weapons higher availability, lower cost, *and* a special benefit compared to other weapons. Why would I spend 2x as much on a "Hand Weapon" when I could instead buy an easier-to-find prybar that also gives me +5% on tinkering?

There are also guns. Hell yeah, love some fantasy with proper guns. They tend to ignore armor, take way longer to reload, and have costly ammo. Sounds about right.

##  Page 22 — Defensive Gear

Physical damage is reduced by the armor you wear. Leather reduces damage by 1, chainmail by 2, and plate by 3. Um. This seems useless at first glance if damage is equal to Will (33 on average). I did skip ahead to see if I was crazy, and it turns out that NPCs have a different structure than PCs when it comes to damage. Enemies may do 5-20 damage. Reducing 5 to 2 with plate is certainly worthwhile, but there are also clearly dangerous enemies who do 20+ damage, enough to kill PCs in 1-2 hits regardless of their armor. I'm not sure I love this design personally since armor has extreme downsides (disadvantage on hiding and running, the other two common solutions to avoiding damage).

## Page 23 — Carrying Capacity

No real carrying capacity rules but if the GM thinks you're carrying too much then you lose Hope. This seems oddly punitive.

## Pages 24 through 31 — How to Play

This is pretty much all the same mechanics from Mothership: stats, saves, advantage, disadvantage, criticals, skills, etc. Gerding added a new "Bit" system where a bit is just the one's place in the d100 roll and several mechanics now trigger off of a "Bit". Ex: fail a Mind save and you may reduce your Hope by the "Bit" on the roll.

A big flavor change is that **Stress** is now **Hope**. If you have less Hope, you're more likely to fail a Hope check and gain a Despair. No idea if these are good yet, but I'll check back in when I get to the Despair section.

## Page 32 — Unnatural Abilities

When you have a high level skill, you can now spend skill points to unlock an unnatural ability. What are they? Well, that's up to you and the Guide. Frankly, I'm disappointed. The text encourages you to create magical abilities evocative of fairytales like a fog whisperer gains the ability to shape the fog at-will to create terrifying images, but doing so reduces the whisperer's Hope.

Why not give us a list of these unnatural abilities to pick from? This is interesting shit thats hard to come up with on the spot, the exact kind of thing I want to buy a book for. I don't need to buy a book so it can tell me that I can give my player a cool ability if I feel like it.

It also includes a note to carefully balance it to prevent "breaking" the experience, which I just find so draining. You can give someone invincibility in a fairytale game and they can still lose their character to an eternal sleep spell, or lose their vision and hearing, or...

"Balance" of these magical abilities isn't a real concern, and when you present it as one you're encouraging a table style of adversarial play.

## Page 39 — Death

When you are reduced to 0 Health, you make a Body Save. On a success, your character gets to live, but Death exacts a toll. There is a huge spread that details this toll and they're a little all over the place. Some are more classic fairytale, some are physical wounds, some are roleplay focused, and some are boring mechanical penalties. Examples:

- "You no longer cast a shadow. Creatures of the Fog always know where you are."
- "Lose 5d10 teeth. More than 32 missing teeth means you die."
- "You are quicker to anger and more impulsive than you once were."
- Lose 1d10 in some stat.

I wish these were all fairytale tolls; the fairytale aspects of Fatherfog are its selling point! I don't need a random table of physical scars or penalties to stats. Make the fairytale game where Death is a character ***feel*** like a fairytale game. Death wants your kid, your shadow, your smile; he doesn't care about your left foot!

## Pages 42 through 46 — The Guide's Section

There is a lot of generic guide advice here, which is fine, but there are some interesting bits regarding dealing damage. The GM never rolls anything, they only determine the damage from a player's successful/failed save. The biggest change from Mothership: even on a successful save, the enemy **never misses**. Instead of taking 30 damage, a play who saves takes a Bit of damage (the one's place from the save roll). 

## Page 47 — Sample Enemies

Now we're getting to some interesting playable content. Enemies have hit points, damage, optional special abilities, motives, and tricks.

- **Hit points:** 2-3 HP with a threshold of 5-15 damage. Do enough damage, the enemy loses 1 HP. This can then trigger them to flee or triggers new attacks/abilities. The text also advises that almost all creatures flee once they fall to 1 HP.
- **Damage:** Enemies deal static damage like `Bow & Arrow (15 + a Bit)`. PCs only take the full damage on a failed save, but they always take the Bit of damage. I expect this to be far faster to stat and run than the Mothership alternative.
- **Special Abilities:** These could be anything, but a key according to the text is that they're usually tied to the enemy's HP. For example, the Cursed Hunter transforms into a wolf beast with stronger attacks when they lose 1 HP. I really like the potential for enemies to dynamically change as a fight drags on.
- **Motives:** A simple explanation for why an enemy might fight you, or why they might not. Can you find a way to avoid violence?
- **Trick:** These are short bits of text that include a weakness or way to resolve an encounter with the enemy without killing them (ex: bribing).

## Pages 48 through 55 — Legacy Artifacts

Best content in the entire zine. One hundred magic items with folklore effects and flavor. Some personal favorites include...

- A **sleep brick** that gives someone the benefit of 4 hours of sleep if you hit them hard enough with it. Also it counts as a thrown weapon, because, well yeah.
- A **bearskin** that turns the wearer into a bear and forces them to live as one for seven years, after which they gain beauty and unlimited wealth.
- The **map crow**; an amenable companion with cartography skills and knowledge.

There's so many more—97 to be exact—and they can be used in any fairytale or dark fantasy game, not just this one!

## Pages 56 through 61 — Despair

A long list of different despairs. As a hater of the panic system from Mothership, this still doesn't suite my fancy. Most of them can be summed up as "Lose 1-3 maximum Hope and gain a niche trigger for some minor roleplay effect like screaming any time you roll a critical failure." I know some people love this kind of shit, but I just don't think the **Hope -> Despair -> less Hope -> Despair more often -> Despair again** pipeline is very interesting. I would much rather have three spreads of unnatural abilities in the zine.

## Layout Concerns

In short, I have a lot. It's difficult to be as critical of the layout as I am without coming across as attacking the designer. So I will mention that I really enjoy the aesthetic aspects of the design, even if the design is inaccessible and lacks clarity.

- A lot of text across the zine is highlighted in this pale green, but since its placed on an off-white background, I really struggled to read it. I checked with a WCAG color contrast checker and it failed every benchmark (contrast ratio of 2.4:1). I get they want it to stand out from the surrounding text, but you can accomplish this without penalizing the visually impaired. As someone with such an impairment, I will take it personally if TKG doesn't change this (I am sending them feedback).
- Justified text in a two column layout might be an OSR staple, but that doesn't mean I don't want to see it taken out back like Ol' Yeller. I already see a future gluttony of zines with atrocious justified text. The [typographic rivers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/River_(typography)) will flow and flood the spreads. The horror; the horror!
- Centering headers in two column formats with inline images that jumble paragraphs creates an effect where the eye has to zig-zag across the page. Just left-align the headers. The english world reads left-to-right anyway. It makes even less sense since the main benefit of justified text is to improve the visual structure of a page at a glance. The columns, the paragraphs, they start and end at clear lines. But when you center the headers, the columns are no longer quadrilateral, they can begin at ragged edges. What's the point?
- There are too many typefaces. Not sure why they chose a harder to read type for the sub-chapter headers. The main header typeface meshes better with the vibe and layout.
- There are clear spacing decisions that were made not for design reasons, but just to cram more text onto the page. The more space between elements, the less related they are. Consider page 27. Why is there more space between the paragraphs than the headers? The "Modifying Stat Checks & Saves" header appears to be attached to the previous body paragraphs, not the paragraphs that follow it. ## Layout Suggestions

- **Remove pale green text:** Italics should be enough to distinguish the text. And if you must insist on a color with lower luminosity, it must still adhere to color contrast guidelines. Although I think the reason the italics don't stand out is because the body font is already so dark and strong and vivid that the italics might get lost in the noise. Right now they're just lost in the background instead. It might seem like I'm being harsh, but I basically couldn't read any of this text in the entire zine (its a lot of the zine).
- **Remove justification:** Remove justification for text frames that span a third or half of the page, but you could keep it for text that spans the entire page. Even then, it likely needs some adjustments, like hyphenation enabled. There are hours of content that will try to teach you how to do justification properly.
- **Left align headers:** Centered headers and large blocks of prose look great in a novel aspect, or in a children's book, but for better or for worse this is designed to be a technical system reference document. Make it easy to reference.
- **Reduce typefaces and styles:** Consider why you are using another typeface for each header. What utility does it bring? What aesthetic does it improve? Is anything really lost if you remove this typeface from the document? Do you need to use 9+ character styles on one page?
- **Adjust header spacing:** An easy rule to follow is that header styles should always have a "space before" value greater than their "space after", and greater than the body style's "space between". This ensures that headers always stay relatively close to their body paragraphs and far from earlier paragraphs they're not related to, creating more cohesive segments of information on a spread.

## TLDR;

- Fatherfog is mechanically near identical to Mothership.
- The biggest mechanical difference is how combat works. PCs always take damage, the Guide never rolls, and the monsters are structured more like puzzles.
- The character creation is still interesting, but it has weaker genre archetypes than Mothership's four core classes, resulting in a somewhat gamey setup. We all remember the "philosopher" from *Into the Woods*, right? It's definitely not just a generic word for the gameplay role of a character with high intellect and focused skills, right?
- The best parts of the zine are the fairytale elements like the cloaks and the legacy artifacts. **If nothing else in the zine is valuable to you, you can always use the legacy artifacts as random magic items for your home games.**
- The zine's layout was a struggle to get through. It is inaccessible and several design choices make the text harder to reference at the table.
- Without any adventures or campaign generators, it is very difficult to play this game as is, but this will likely change in the near future.
