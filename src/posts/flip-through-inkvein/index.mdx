_Flip Throughs are an excuse for me to set aside some time to read through a new RPG. They are not deep dives or formal analysis. I like to point out what pushes me to read further, what elements I think put me off on playing the game, and overall if it's a good idea for you to read it yourself. I don't edit them, so if there are mistakes I'm not liable._

---

_Inkvein_ is a new MÖRK BORG megadungeon from ENNIE-nominated writer [Murkdice](https://murkdice.substack.com/) and ENNIE-Award winning publisher [Space Penguin Ink](https://spacepenguin.ink/). Their [launch on BackerKit](https://www.backerkit.com/c/projects/space-penguin-ink/inkvein-a-caving-centric-megadungeon-for-mork-borg) is already a success, raising over $30k at the time of writing. As part of their promotion, they released a ~20 page quickstart. I love when designers do this because it allows me to really see what I am backing. I've backed projects where they put out some great initial art and one fantastic spread, but when the game arrives on my doorstep it falls far from that initial vision. There's no regrets with a quickstart.

## Page 1 — Cover

The cover is fine, but I wish the lines on the knight art were grimier. 

## Page 2 — Hyperlinks

> This document is hyperlinked. Page numbers and location numbers (including those on maps) jump to the relevant pages.

I respect the hell out of this. Many OSR games with huge locations and byzantine keying systems lack all bookmarks and linking within their PDFs. It's a really useful tool and should be the default for accessibility. The fact that they put in the effort to accomplish this for the quickstart shows promise.

## Page 3 — “What would you give for a splash of Ink?”

A piece of art covers the full page with some text to set the stage for Inkvein. The only problem I have with this is that I'm still not quite sure what Ink is for. It has power, power enough to convince people to brave dangerous, claustrophobic caverns, but what does it actually do? Maybe the author assumes this is answered already for anyone who picked the book up—or by the BackerKit page—but I did not read the BackerKit page once I saw there was a quickstart. I'm intrigued, but it feels a bit too mysterious, even coy, for me.

## Pages 4 + 5 — An actual quickstart

Everything you need to setup and run a simple _Inkvein_ game. It's great to see an actual quickstart that gives you almost everything you need to know on one spread. The layout disappoints me a bit. Its not bad, its trad. At least for OSR. Two columns, serif text, lots of bolding. But look, maybe I'm old-fashioned, but you don't need to bold most words in a bulleted list. People can just decide to read it or not based on the header. They either know what is in the quickstart, or they want to know. **Bolding** the word **caving** does not make **caving** easier to reference it just means that the author wants me to know that **caving** is important and I need to remember **caving**.

- ✅ Alternative character creation focused on climbing/mining
- ✅ Relatively simple time and light tracking procedure

## Pages 6 + 7 — Ooh caves

Awesome spread. It intersperses rules text with characters tunneling deep into tight spaces and struggling to climb vertical passages. The section on Claustrophobia is well-done, and vital considering how much attention there is to squeezing your way through tight routes. The climbing mishaps table is probably the best bit of actual game design. There are 8 results, but the last 2 are the only ones that deal considerable damage. If you have a climbing kit, you only roll a d6 on the table instead of a d8. So as long as your climbing kit is working, you will be pretty safe even if the worst happens. But most mishaps deal damage in time or equipment loss, which can be quite damaging across the scope of a megadungeon.

## Pages 8 + 9 — Beasts galore

I like the creatures, especially the Letterwings. The bestiary art almost reminds me of some early Mothership stuff. I'm not a visual artist, so I don't really know the medium, but it looks sufficiently wet and inky. However, I must point out some further unnecessary bolding (the OSR's greatest sin):

While "They hunger for written words" stands on its own, the phrase "If no written words are to be found..." does not. So what can we deduce from this? The bolding is to drive our attention to the the start of the new paragraph, nothing more. Looking across the rest of the spread, we can see this virtue applied unilaterally. Here's the thing, the whitespace breaking up the paragraphs already achieves the same effect. It takes ~30 seconds to read most of these descriptions and even less than that to read the stat block alone. I don't think the bolding aids readability OR referable text properties.

## Pages 10 + 11 — Sequential Encounters

Hell yeah. I love sequential encounters. It almost forces the writer to engage with proper environment storytelling and lends itself to building up a narrative arc. From a gameplay perspective it gives players tools to potentially avoid or prepare for dangerous encounters, similar to omens in some systems.

## Page 11 — Swashes

Okay so I have to say I am over the [swashes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Swash_(typography)).

<Figure src={swash}
        alt="A paragraph from Inkvein where each word that ends in 'e' has a huge, disruptive swash. There are at least ten swashes over the course of three sentences."
        caption="Did you know lots of words end in 'e'? Would you like to see how often? Add a swash today!" />

This is too many swashes. Please just put two max per paragraph, hopefully in just the most important parts. If only the word "note" at the top of the sidebar had the swash it would look fine, but across the whole sidebar makes me actively not want to read it. Some people will think this is not that important to the quality of the game and not worth blogging about, and they might be right, but have they considered that I really don't like all these swashes?

## Page 12 — Maps are cool

I like that the map indicates crawl vs tight vs open routes. Light rules have to be stated I guess, but I would have assumed all of these anyway. The "Ink-Things" section is actually some of the coolest diagetic rules in the whole quickstart and there's no bolding to be seen. What a glorious day.

## Page 13 through 20 — Adventure Time

I don't want to cover the details of each page of the adventure in case anyone wants to run it. However, I will say that I enjoy how many characters are included within the 16 keyed locations. A lot of OSR dungeons are empty, save for monsters. Not true for Inkvein. There are people who want to hurt you, people who want your help, and people who are beyond your help but may aid you anyway. There are also some cool environmental hazards created out of Ink, which makes me think that Ink will be more than a theme or motif and have a consistent, mechanistic impact on your game. 

## Page 21 — Time Tracking

And finally, a neat little sheet for tracking time with fountain nibs. Inky. I think it would be nice to include some pregenerated characters in the back, but ah it's easy enough to roll up a MÖRK BORG character.

---

This isn't a proper review and the adventure is only in quickstart form for now, but I think it's pretty good! Most of the aspects that ticked me off are commonplace amongst successful OSR games these days so no reason that it should hold this one back. The twisting narratives in the sequential encounter table combined with the rest of the written adventure is excellent. If you're enticed by the idea of climbing in the dark with hungry, inky hounds below you, check out [Inkvein, crowdfunding now!](https://www.backerkit.com/c/projects/space-penguin-ink/inkvein-a-caving-centric-megadungeon-for-mork-borg).
