## Preamble, or why Vermis is a TTRPG

For the uninitiated, [Vermis](https://hollow-press.net/products/vermis-i) is an artbook series by Plastiboo inspired by old dungeon-crawling games. Each book in the series is an "official guide of a game that doesn't exist". The aesthetic falls somewhere between video game, game guide, and choose your own adventure novels.

I sometimes tell people that Vermis is my favorite tabletop roleplaying game; in large part because I think it can spawn interesting discussion. It is truly one of my favorite books to flip through when planning fantasy sessions. Isn't it just pretty though? An art piece meant to collect dust on a shelf? I mean, can you really run a session of Vermis?

**YES.** Many may disagree, but as someone who enjoys the blackbox or HUDless style of running games, any detailed piece of fiction is fruitful for gameplay. Most might find it difficult to run a campaign solely with a copy of "The Silmarillion", but Vermis presents a unique opportunity. The text mimics old-school video games, many of which in turn mimicked old-school tabletop RPGs. It contains:

- **Random monsters** you might encounter on your travels.
- **Regions** with connected points of interest.
- **Points of interest** with encounters and NPCs.
- **Encounters** with rewards.
- **NPCs** with quests.

This sounds like an old school game to me. So what is it missing? Stats? How about you [just use bears, or wolves, or dragons?](https://dicegoblin.blog/just-use-bears-or-wolves-dragons-or-spiders/) In fact, why don't you just use the free [Cairn 2E](https://cairnrpg.com/second-edition/) and whichever of these [animal statblocks](https://bommyknocker.bearblog.dev/universal-encounter-table#cairn-statblocks) seems the most appropriate.

Great, now that that's sorted, we just need one last thing to play Vermis: a map. And that's the point of this series. I'm going to make you a map.
