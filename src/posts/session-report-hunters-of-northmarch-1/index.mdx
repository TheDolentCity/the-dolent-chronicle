This game is set in the region of Northmarch, a far-off settlement of a Byzantine Empire. Players are **Hunters**, supernatural mutants trained for one purpose: killing monsters. While I am creating system-neutral documents for this campaign, I am running it using D&D 4th edition. The game wiki can be found online: [Hunters of Northmarch Wiki.](https://hunters-of-northmarch.vercel.app/)

There are four player characters:

- **Claudia** (she/her), a shifter with cat eyes who wears simple cloth and uses her connection to divine powers to hunt down foes with her massive battleaxe.
- **Girf** (he/him), a bat-eared eladrin covered in hides who uses his connection to nature to transform into terrifying swarms of bats.
- **Keen** (he/him), a cat-eyed elf who uses his connection to the power of creation to enhance arms and armor.
- **Londum** (he/him), a heavily-armored dwarf with bat ears who works with divine runes to craft powerful magic and heal his allies.

## Session Report

### Rats in the basement

The session began with the players meeting for the first time. A member of the Hunter's Guild gave them their first assignment. A tax collector and his imperial patrol went missing near the lead mining town of Delfan. The guild isn't sure if this means anything, sometimes there are deserters, but the band of hunters was sent to investigate either way.

They soon found themselves at the Red Rose Tavern where everyone but the barkeep was terrified of their appearance—no one wants to see hunters in their town. The halfling barkeep introduced himself as Old Bill, told them they hadn't seen any imperials, and offered them food and lodging if they killed the giant rats in his basement 🥁.

### A Quiet Town

They agreed (begrudgingly) and quickly exterminated the 50lb vermin. The hunters discovered a crawl tunnel that the rats had made that lead somewhere north into town. Perhaps towards the mine they saw when on their approach? When they surfaced from the basement, the tavern was cleared out; even Old Bill was gone. They proceeded to search the rest of town and found it dead; no sound or movement at all. The Druid Girf even noticed that there were no birds, insects, or beasts of any kind in the town or surrounding forest.

They came across loose sheets of paper in the wind that led to the wide open door of the Apothecary. There were shattered potions, torn books, broken glass, and more concerning, deep claw marks in the wooden walls and floor. The rear door was sealed with some kind of weak magic, but before they could crack it open they heard a cry from somewhere on the north side of town.

### Mine Crawl

Throwing caution to the wind, they raced over and found evidence of a struggle right outside of the mine. They determined it was time to head in, even though they didn't have a clue what was happening. What proceeded was a classic dungeon crawl. Within the dark confines of the mine they first came across a drowned passageway with an imperial boot poking out of the water; the body of the tax collector. They began to hear chanting and determined it was some kind of ritual, but couldn't be sure what it was, so they laid low.

Keen scouted ahead and found a wooden pen of some kind, as he peered into the dark with his limited darkvision, he found a group of townsfolk bound and gagged. From their appearance (and smell) they had been this way for days. Then out of the dark, two yellow eyes met his. A vile creature issued forth from the pen. It walked on two legs like a humanoid, but it was covered in short, dark fur. It had clawed fingers and toes. Most disturbingly, it wore shredded human clothes, but where a humanoid head should have been there was instead the head of a rat.

The creature leapt and bit Keen, but was soon slain. The band of hunters reconvened and determined that they would save these townsfolk before stopping the ritual. The villagers were unbound and led out of the mine with the help of the hunters. Then the ritual chanting stopped, and not even a moment to breathe had passed before a roar shook the entire mine. A new creature was born.

### Vermunculus

The roaring creature soon attacked and they saw its horrific visage in the firelight. It was twice the height of a man, and wider than two wagons. The monster was built like a gorilla, with massive oversized arms that it used to run and walk. This monster, like the other, bore a rodent head, but not all of its flesh was rodent-like; the skin had clear seems where different fleshes had been stitched together. This wasn't some monster conjured by a Dusk God in times past, this was an abomination made by some dark sorcerer.

As the fight progressed, the four hunters dealt strong blows to the creature, but more of those original rat-headed people and the sorceress herself arrived. The shifter, Claudia, was struck a heavy blow by Vermunculus and nearly killed. She gained [a ropy scar](https://hunters-of-northmarch.vercel.app/dnd-4th-edition/rules/scars/) across her back for her trouble. Her wounds were quickly sealed with the help of a divine rune, and she reemerged to kill Vermunculus. But even in pieces, the flesh of the monster continued to try and bind itself back together.

The rat-headed monsters were quickly dispatched and even though the sorceress fled before that happened, Claudia was able to catch up to her and kill her before she could truly escape. The band of hunters emerged successful, but in truth, had little idea what had occurred here.

### Conclusion + Downtime

As it was late, I called the session here and had the players make rolls in a play-by-post manner to resolve what mystery they could. While they did find evidence of an apothecary who created "rodenthropes" they still understood little about the disease. **They are still uncertain if they have contracted the disease themselves.**

I have added a [downtime system](https://hunters-of-northmarch.vercel.app/dnd-4th-edition/rules/downtime/) to D&D 4th edition, based on several games such as Lancer, Trespasser, and uh I forget what other stuff I looked at, but I tailored it to "monster hunting" so while its a bit storygamey I think it will work well.

## Postmortem

### What went well?
- I adopted a "kill, solve, save" as a tweaked form of the paradigm from Mothership. In this case, the band of hunters killed the monster and saved (most) of the townsfolk. If they spent more time solving the mystery, they likely wouldn't have saved the townsfolk.
- I ran a combat with four level 1 PCs, four rodenthropes, one Vermunculus, and one sorceress in about ~1.5 hours. I think this is pretty good considering the amount of HP the enemies had and the fact that it was everyone's first time playing in a while (or ever). 
- The players worked together quite well, both in and out of combat. They split off when they deemed it appropriate, asked each other's opinions, and gave bonuses or aid to each other on their turns. They even split up the four magic items they found without any biting or yelling.
- Two players independent of each other started writing in-character journals, which is always cool to see.

### What went poorly?
- When the ritual is completed, Vermunculus is born and chases down the hunters. At that point in time, the hunters were helping the villagers out of the entrance. Since Vermunculus had two initiative slots and high speed, it outsped all of its allies and fought the hunters 1 vs 4 for several rounds until its allies showed up. This was not only easier on the hunters, but not as interesting from a D&D 4th edition gameplay point-of-view. **I chose the world logic over the encounter balance.** I don't know if this is right or wrong to have done for this weird hybrid style of game I'm running, but it was a point of criticism from at least one player.
- The hunters performed very little investigation. I did put some time pressure on them; according to my prep clocks, two characters are kidnapped at some point early in the adventure. Maybe this is an adventure flaw, maybe the players don't care about mysteries too much. In this instance, it didn't matter as much because the monsters were low-level and "simpler" since this was one player's first time playing 4e and one other has little 4e experience. In the coming sessions, I expect to have monsters that require more fairytale-logic to defeat.

### Changes I'm making
No real changes right now. I will be having fewer locations to explore in each session, partially for time, partially because I think its unnecessary to prep as many as I did with the time span of these sessions.
