## The Legend of the Great Hunting Oak

```
Written by Dante Nardo
Editing by Dante Nardo, Bridget Estelle, and J Alan Henning
```

Once upon a time, raiders attacked and set fire to a small village on the edge of a forest. The flames spread to the nearby trees. Only a farmer escaped the destruction. He fled deep into the woods and collapsed at the foot of the old oak tree. Blood from his belly seeped over his hand and onto the tree's roots. The farmer begged his gods for help. He begged his ancestors for help. He begged the tree for help. As the sun set, and his blood and tears fell upon the roots, a creature emerged from the trunk of the tree itself. His visage was that of a man ten meters tall, skin all of browned bark with a burl for a beard. Autumnal leaves from the oak descended and draped themselves as a hooded cloak across his shoulders. With a snap, a curved tree limb as tall as the bark-skinned man himself fell from the oak. With just a touch, he transmuted it into a great bow. The farmer was too weak to shout or run, so he leaned away from the tree with a silent scream on his face. The bark-skinned man reached out with his rootish fingers and touched the farmer, filling his mind with visions of a great hunt and the raiders who attacked his home impaled by arrows as big as tree limbs. As the vision faded, the farmer watched a dozen wooden puppets walk out from the woods. Dusty spores rained down from the treetops and settled onto the farmer's wound. His bleeding stilled as bark grew over the wound. The bark spread and spread, until only another wooden puppet remained. It joined the ranks of the others and shambled towards the smell of smoke.

## The Legend of the Night Children

```
Written by Dante Nardo
Editing by Dante Nardo, Bridget Estelle, and J Alan Henning
```

Once upon a time, a brother and sister lived with their father at the foot of a mountain. Often, he would leave them alone for days to go hunting. Their father gave them, as his father gave him, three rules they must follow while he was gone: don't go up the mountain alone, don't go out after dark, and cover your ears if you hear music. One twilight, their father out hunting, the brother heard the most beautiful singing coming from the mountain. His sister called him inside, but the brother wouldn't listen and ran towards the music. The sister, remembering the first rule, followed him up the mountain. When the sister caught up to her brother, she found he had cut his knee and was walking slower. She wanted to turn back, but her brother wanted to find the singer first. The song reverberated from the mouth of a nearby cave. It was very loud now. As the children crossed the threshold of the cave, the sun set, and the music stopped. It was quite dark, and it took their eyes a moment to adjust. The face of another child emerged from the shadows. The brother walked forward to meet the child, but the figure moved into the last of the fading sunlight, revealing their monstrous form. The creature was at least three times taller than the children, with short, black fur and large bat wings. It peered at the brother's bloody knee and began to sing. The melody was sad and wondrous at the same time, and the sister exclaimed as the wound closed of its own accord. She thanked the creature and turned to leave, but her brother remained still as stone. He was older, and bigger, and try as she might she could not drag him away. The song grew louder as other creatures in the cave joined in. The sister covered her ears and watched as her brother grew taller. Black fur sprouted from his skin. His arms twisted, and bones cracked, as they turned into wings. But his face remained serene. The sister fled and hid in the cottage until her father returned. She never told anyone what happened to her brother, but she gave the three rules to her children one day too. On some nights, she still sees her brother's face soaring in the night sky.

## The Legend of the Feathered Serpent

```
Written by Dante Nardo
Editing by Dante Nardo, Bridget Estelle, and J Alan Henning
```

Once upon a time, there was a struggling hamlet in the high desert. Two ravens, obsidian-feathered paramours, would appear to the people of the hamlet as omens of ill fate. The townsfolk would fortify their homes, fill larders in secure rooms beneath their floors, and gather days’ worth of water in buckets. Sandstorms, raiders, and other disasters were avoided in this manner. But the people grew tired. Life was hard and some families fled or fell apart. Those who remained began to resent the ravens. One luckless eve, a young villager threw a rock at one of the ravens. It fell to the earth, dead. The other raven cried out with a piercing screech and descended on the young villager who had killed its mate. With its beak, it tore the eyes from the stone thrower's face. It swallowed them and grew in size. It found another villager who had run towards the screaming and ate their eyes as well. It ate and ate and grew and grew until it was a raven no longer, but a wyrm, legless with sparse inky feathers. The corpulent serpent had grown longer and taller than the hamlet itself. It took flight clumsily, smashing homes as it flew into the setting sun. Wounds opened on the serpent’s side and viridian blood fell into the village's well, poisoning the water. And so the town was left blind and desolated. The survivors pilgrimaged to a neighboring village, but refused food, drink, and speech. They could only rave about the black serpent at the edge of the horizon.
