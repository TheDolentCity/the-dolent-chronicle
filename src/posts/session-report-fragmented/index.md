I began playing tabletop roleplaying games in college and my first table always invested extra work into "Halloween" sessions. Sometimes silly, sometimes horrific, each year we found ourselves trying to outdo the previous experiences. When that campaign ended, we still committed to Halloween sessions with different game systems.

This year's Halloween session took place in March because we are all busy adults and two of us had to coordinate travel to New York City. The game: [**DREAD**](<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dread_(role-playing_game)>). If you're unfamiliar, the core mechanic requires you to pull blocks from a Jenga Tower to overcome obstacles or avoid danger. If the tower falls, your character DIES (or is removed from the game). It's a compelling premise, even if the rules text itself is disappointing [(see this video essay for details)](https://youtu.be/apBz4IrjqBA?si=0BxYtX4-Rq7brmys). But the Game Master had a few ideas on how he wanted this game to be different from normal Dread.

## Fragmented

The GM titled the session _Fragmented_. Instead of each of us filling out a questionnaire to build a character backstory, we were building a backstory for another random player. Each player then began the game with no memory of who they were beyond their name and appearance.

As the game went on, we would regain our memories. Each one was a cut-out answer to a question from the questionnaire. I thought this would largely be a bit of a gimmick, but it turned out to be a big deal.

## The Session

> _Whelp. It would seem you've been eaten. WHOLE. Hardly even gnawed on in fact, wagon and company included. The beast must have been gargantuan, to manage this whole group without even chewing. You wish you could remember, but oddly enough the memory of the encounter somehow eludes you. This must be the neck of a dragon, judging by the way the pink fleshy walls of the gaping corridor seem to extend for miles to your left and your right. The immediate vicinity is crowded with what appears to be the ruins of a village, possibly even more than one. This particular dragon doesn't seem too picky about what it eats, you muse as the involuntary rippling of peristalsis slowly moves it all (and you) further into the beast's gullet. You glance at the other passengers riding along with you and realize with a jolt that you don't recognize any of them. In fact, you don't even remember getting on this wagon in the first place. Why would you get on a wagon? You don't travel, do you? And while we're at it, who even are you, anyway? You'd ask one of these strangers if they knew, but they all share your befuddled expression; it seems they're in the same wagon as you in more ways than one…_

This is the text that we were greeted with at the start of the game. I learned that I was **Sir Lars the Breaker**, an incredibly muscular man wearing exceedingly tight clothing. The rest of the cast included a manacled dwarf, cloaked and hooded figure, and a dark-haired woman.

As we set off, we came across some crystals. If the right character touched the crystal, it would dissolve and reveal an item, building, or person related to one of our memories. For me, this included finding a mace, my brother, and my beloved tailor's home.

The session was composed almost entirely of these trials and dramatic reveals, until we all had our memories back and arrived at the stomach. As we stood in the esophagus of this beast, we saw dozens and dozens of other openings. I concluded we were in a hydra, but I was only partially correct. A melodic tune was reverberating throughout the stomach, but we had no time to process this as hostile memory fragments attacked us. We smashed the remaining crystals that held this creature together with several pulls from the tower. It was on the verge of collapse, but we survived.

We escaped as the space collapsed around us, and crawled out of the mouth of the beast. We then saw the monster in full as it writhed and died. It had dozens of serpentine necks, each ending with a pale, human face. For the duration of the session, we had been trapped inside of a [False Hydra](https://goblinpunch.blogspot.com/2014/09/false-hydra.html).

## Feedback

The weakest aspect of the session was the terrain and lack of choice. It was an on-the-rails experience. When we first began the session, several players concocted a plan to get to the stomach as fast as possible to try and force the monster to throw us up. It soon became clear we would be in this infinite tunnel until the memories were all expired. Players were presented with few obstacles as we could "run away" from most of our memories. It became more enjoyable as an exploration of character scenes. For example, I tried my hardest to cast a fireball during a "wizard exam" memory that another character triggered. I failed, destroying my finely tailored pants. It didn't move the plot forward or resolve any problems, but it was funny enough at the time.

The strongest aspect of the session was the horror or joy of receiving a memory from the GM. Instead of finding loot, we discovered more of ourselves. Some of the characters were quite silly, leading to the group laughing at a member's incredibly befuddled expressions as they read their memory. Others had more dramatic reveals, changing our perspective on the current situation. The memory of my brother (sort of like a ghost) had been with our party for about an hour when he suddenly didn't want to proceed forward. I received a memory that in context led me to assume he had betrayed me in the past, so I tricked him and struck him in the head with my mace, destroying his memory. I then unlocked my final memory, wherein _I betrayed him_. It was a perfect setup and reveal, leaving Sir Lars lonely and devastated.

## END

While the on-the-rails experience led to a few slow moments, it was still a great few hours with friends. I think it would be quite difficult to run a game with amnesiacs and have a more freeform game, but if anyone has done so, I would love to hear about it.
